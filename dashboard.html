Physiological Analysis

📷 G-Force Effects on Cardiovascular Response (Phase 2: 10–20 min)

During rocket launch, the astronaut experiences G-forces increasing from 1g to ~3g. This creates a significant cardiovascular challenge: gravitational pooling forces blood toward the lower extremities, reducing venous return to the heart. The body compensates via the baroreceptor reflex — heart rate surges (observed peak: ~156 bpm), systolic blood pressure spikes (~121 mmHg), and epinephrine floods the bloodstream driving increased cardiac output. The BioGears DataRequests clearly capture this acute sympathetic response.

🌞 Fluid Redistribution in Microgravity (Phases 3–4: 20–50 min)

Upon entering microgravity, the hydrostatic gradient that normally pools blood in the legs disappears. Blood redistributes headward (cephalad fluid shift), increasing central venous pressure and causing a puffy-face sensation. The kidneys detect this apparent "hypervolemia" and begin excreting fluid, gradually reducing total blood volume. Heart rate and blood pressure normalize and may drop slightly below Earth baseline as cardiac preload is reduced. SpO2 recovers to normal after launch stress.

🏠 ISS Stabilization (Phase 5: 50–60 min)

Within the pressurized, temperature-controlled ISS environment (101 kPa, 21% O2, 22°C), the astronaut reaches a new physiological equilibrium. Heart rate stabilizes ~81 bpm (slightly below Earth baseline due to reduced gravitational demands on the heart), blood pressure remains slightly lower, and SpO2 is maintained at 97–98%. Core temperature returns to normal. The BioGears simulation confirms successful environmental adaptation.
